Scanning the World

Lately, I've been obsessed with volumetrically scanning objects in nature. There's something profound about capturing a physical object from a place; a three dimensional snapshot.

For now, this means carefully hovering around rocks and trees with my iPhone. A few apps are able to take advantage of the depth sensor used for Face ID. It's nascent and clunky technology, but it taps into something deeply human: the desire to hold onto a souvenir from an important moment.

When placed in virtual and augmented reality, these become incredibly interesting. The technology opens up entirely new possibilities for assemblage art, and might redefine how we collect and display meaningful objects.

The author finding beauty in a rock at Big Sur. You can see the results in the gallery above.

I'm still experimenting, but have had success in visualizing and manipulating scanned items and textures with the Microsoft HoloLens.

Non-Linear Storyboards

I'd an idea for a narrative AR experience, but faced a problem. How do you write a script that allows for user choice and agency?

A few false starts led me to Twine, an amazing environment for writing interactive fiction. Think old school Choose Your Own Adventure books.

Very much in progress, I've only a few screenshots to share. Designed in the style of a silent film, I plan to replace the descriptions with illustrations and use the finished interactive storyboard to inform an immersive work.

Twine uses a bespoke markup and scripting language meant to be approachable to non-developers. More than simply helping me realize an idea, it has rekindled an interest in coding.

In the Darkroom

Photography isn't a new idea or project, it's a constant.

The camera has always blurred the lines between art, science and technology. I don't know how many times I've developed a roll of film or made a print, but it's always magical.

I share a studio and darkroom in the Excelsior neighborhood of San Francisco, on one of the most surveilled intersections in the city. Nearby lamp posts bristle with police cameras. If you're in the area, you're being recorded.

I'm interested in exploring and commenting on this surveillance by coupling computer vision with analog photography.
